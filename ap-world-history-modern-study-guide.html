📋 Exam Format, Themes & Skills
Exam Structure: 3 hours 15 minutes total

Section I, Part A: 55 multiple-choice questions (40% of score, 55 min). Sets of 3-4 questions per stimulus (primary texts, secondary texts, images, charts, maps). At least one set uses paired sources.

Section I, Part B: 3 short-answer questions (20% of score, 40 min). Q1 = secondary source (required, 1200-2001). Q2 = primary source (required, 1200-2001). Choose Q3 (1200-1750) or Q4 (1750-2001), no stimulus. Each SAQ has parts a, b, c. No thesis needed.

Section II, Q1: Document-Based Question with 7 documents (25% of score, 60 min including 15-min reading period). Must write a thesis, use at least 6 docs, provide outside evidence, do sourcing (HAPP) on at least 3 docs, and demonstrate complex understanding.

Section II, Q2/3/4: Long Essay, choose 1 of 3 options (15% of score, 40 min). Must write a thesis, support with specific evidence, use a reasoning process (comparison, causation, or CCOT), and demonstrate complex understanding.

Six Course Themes

ENV Humans and the Environment: How the environment shapes societies and how growing populations reshape their environments. Includes disease, agriculture, climate, and resource competition.
CDI Cultural Developments & Interactions: How ideas, beliefs, and religions develop and how their interactions produce political, social, and cultural consequences. Includes art, literature, philosophy, syncretic traditions.
GOV Governance: How states form, expand, and decline. How governments maintain order through administrative institutions. How power is obtained, retained, and exercised.
ECN Economic Systems: How societies produce, exchange, and consume goods and services. Includes trade networks, labor systems, mercantilism, capitalism, and economic imperialism.
SIO Social Interactions & Organization: How societies group their members and how norms governing interactions influence political, economic, and cultural institutions. Includes class, caste, race, gender.
TEC Technology & Innovation: How human adaptation and innovation lead to increased efficiency and security. How technological advances shape human development with both intended and unintended consequences.

Three Reasoning Processes (Used on Every FRQ)

ComparisonCausationContinuity & Change Over Time
Describe similarities and/or differences between historical developments. Explain the reasons for those similarities/differences. Explain the significance of those similarities/differences. Describe causes and/or effects of a development. Explain the relationship between causes and effects. Distinguish primary vs. secondary causes and short-term vs. long-term effects. Explain how context influenced a development. Describe patterns of continuity and/or change over time. Explain why those patterns occurred. Explain the significance of specific developments within a larger pattern of continuity and/or change.

Strategy: HAPP for Document Sourcing
Historical situation (What was going on when this was written?), Audience (Who was this for?), Purpose (Why was it created?), Point of view (What perspective does the author bring?). You need HAPP for at least 3 documents on the DBQ to earn the sourcing point.

Manchu (Qing Dynasty)Non-Han Chinese dynasty (1644-1912) from Manchuria that conquered Ming China; maintained Confucian bureaucracy while imposing Manchu cultural practices (queue hairstyle)
Maroon SocietiesCommunities of escaped enslaved persons in the Caribbean and Brazil who established independent settlements and resisted colonial authorities
MercantilismEconomic theory that national power depends on accumulating wealth (gold/silver) through favorable trade balances and colonial exploitation; governments regulated trade to benefit the mother country
Millet SystemOttoman system organizing non-Muslim communities (Greek Orthodox, Armenian, Jewish) into semi-autonomous groups that governed their own internal affairs
Mit'aIncan labor system requiring subjects to work for the state for a set period each year; adapted by the Spanish to force indigenous labor in silver mines (especially Potosi)
Mughal EmpireMuslim dynasty ruling most of the Indian subcontinent (1526-1857); known for religious tolerance (under Akbar), Taj Mahal, and zamindar tax system
Ottoman EmpireTurkish Islamic empire (c. 1299-1922) spanning Southeast Europe, Western Asia, and North Africa; used devshirme, millet system, and tax farming to govern diverse populations
Protestant Reformation16th-century religious movement begun by Martin Luther (1517) that challenged Catholic Church authority; led to new Christian denominations (Lutheranism, Calvinism, Anglicanism)
Pueblo Revolt (1680)Uprising by Pueblo people in New Mexico against Spanish colonial rule; temporarily expelled Spanish for 12 years; response to forced religious conversion and labor exploitation
Safavid EmpirePersian empire (1501-1736) that established Shi'a Islam as the state religion; rival of the Sunni Ottoman Empire; known for art, architecture, and trade
SikhismMonotheistic religion founded by Guru Nanak in 15th-century Punjab; synthesized elements of Hinduism and Islam; rejected caste system
SyncreticBlending elements from different religions, cultures, or philosophies into a new form; common result of cultural contact (e.g., Vodou, Santeria)
Tax FarmingOttoman practice of auctioning the right to collect taxes in a region to private individuals (tax farmers) who kept a portion as profit
Tokugawa ShogunateMilitary government ruling Japan (1603-1868); enforced strict social hierarchy, limited foreign trade (sakoku policy), and maintained internal peace for 250+ years
Triangular TradeAtlantic trade pattern connecting Europe (manufactured goods), Africa (enslaved persons), and the Americas (raw materials like sugar, tobacco, cotton)
ZamindarMughal tax collectors/landholders responsible for collecting revenue from peasants in their districts; served as intermediaries between the state and rural populations

🌍 Unit 1: The Global Tapestry (c. 1200-1450) 8-10%

1.1 Developments in East Asia

GOV CDI ECN

Song Dynasty China maintained rule through Confucianism and an imperial bureaucracy with civil service examinations that selected officials based on merit. Neo-Confucianism blended traditional Confucian ethics with Buddhist and Daoist metaphysics, becoming the dominant intellectual framework.

Cultural influence spread outward: Filial piety, Neo-Confucianism, and Buddhism reached Korea and Heian Japan. Chinese literary and scholarly traditions were adopted and adapted. Confucian traditions included expectations of deference from women, though elite women in Song China had more economic freedom than is sometimes assumed.

Buddhism continued to shape Asian societies through three major branches: Theravada (dominant in Southeast Asia and Sri Lanka, focused on monastic life and individual enlightenment), Mahayana (dominant in East Asia, emphasized universal salvation through bodhisattvas), and Tibetan (blended Buddhist philosophy with indigenous Bon practices).

The Song economy was the most commercialized in the world: Champa rice from Vietnam allowed double-cropping and population growth. The Grand Canal connected northern and southern China. Iron and steel production expanded dramatically. Artisans produced textiles and porcelains specifically for export markets. Paper money and early banking practices facilitated commerce. The economy depended on free peasant and artisanal labor rather than coerced labor.

1.2 Developments in Dar al-Islam

CDI GOV TEC

Islam, Judaism, and Christianity all continued to shape societies across Africa and Asia. Islam was the dominant organizing force throughout the Dar al-Islam, but religious minorities often maintained their communities.

As the Abbasid Caliphate fragmented, new Islamic political entities emerged, most dominated by Turkic peoples: the Seljuk Empire (Central/Western Asia), the Mamluk Sultanate of Egypt (ruled by formerly enslaved soldiers who defeated the Mongols at Ain Jalut in 1260), and the Delhi Sultanates (five successive Muslim dynasties in northern India from 1206-1526). These states all demonstrated continuity (maintaining Islamic governance traditions), innovation (new administrative practices), and diversity (adapting to local conditions).

Muslim rule expanded through military expansion AND through the activities of merchants, missionaries, and especially Sufis (mystics who emphasized personal spiritual experience, making Islam accessible and appealing to local populations).

Intellectual innovations flourished: Nasir al-Din al-Tusi made advances in mathematics and astronomy. 'A'ishah al-Ba'uniyyah contributed to Islamic literature. Medical knowledge advanced significantly. The House of Wisdom in Abbasid Baghdad had preserved and commented on Greek moral and natural philosophy. Scholarly and cultural transfers occurred between Muslim and Christian Spain (Al-Andalus), transmitting classical knowledge back to Europe.

1.3 South and Southeast Asia

CDI GOV

Hinduism, Islam, and Buddhism continued to shape societies. The Bhakti movement in Hinduism emphasized personal devotion to a deity through love and song, sometimes challenging caste distinctions. Sufism spread Islam through personal connection. Buddhist monasticism remained important in Southeast Asia.

New Hindu and Buddhist states emerged and demonstrated diversity: Vijayanagara Empire (Hindu kingdom in southern India, major trading center), Srivijaya Empire (Buddhist maritime empire in Southeast Asia controlling Strait of Malacca trade), Rajput kingdoms (Hindu warrior states in northwestern India), Khmer Empire (Hindu-Buddhist kingdom, built Angkor Wat, sophisticated irrigation), Majapahit (Hindu-Buddhist kingdom in Java), Sukhothai kingdom (Thai Buddhist state), Sinhala dynasties (Buddhist rulers of Sri Lanka).

1.4 State Building in the Americas

GOV

American states demonstrated continuity, innovation, and diversity and expanded in scope and reach, independent of Afro-Eurasian developments.

Maya city-states: Decentralized political system with competing city-states; advanced mathematics (concept of zero), astronomy, writing (hieroglyphics), and calendar systems.

Mexica (Aztec) Empire: Centered at Tenochtitlan (population ~200,000+); used a tribute system to extract wealth from conquered peoples; practiced human sacrifice for religious legitimacy; chinampas (floating gardens) for intensive agriculture.

Inca Empire: Largest pre-Columbian empire in the Americas, stretching along western South America; mit'a labor system required subjects to work for the state; quipu (knotted strings) for record-keeping; extensive road network (~25,000 miles) connecting the empire; no written language or wheeled vehicles.

North American societies: Chaco and Mesa Verde (Pueblo cultures with multi-story stone/adobe buildings in the Southwest), Cahokia (major Mississippian mound-building center near modern St. Louis, peak population ~20,000-40,000).

1.5 State Building in Africa

GOV

African states demonstrated continuity, innovation, and diversity and expanded in scope and reach.

Great Zimbabwe: Stone-walled city in southeastern Africa (peaked ~1300); controlled gold trade between the interior and Swahili Coast ports; impressive architecture without mortar.

Ethiopia: Christian kingdom in East Africa; maintained independence from Muslim neighbors; Lalibela's rock-hewn churches demonstrated religious devotion and architectural innovation.

Hausa Kingdoms: City-states in West Africa (modern Nigeria/Niger); major centers of trans-Saharan trade, manufacturing (leather, cloth), and Islamic scholarship.

1.6 Developments in Europe

CDI GOV SIO

Christianity, Judaism, and Islam all shaped European societies. The Catholic Church was the most powerful institution in Western Europe, unifying diverse kingdoms through shared faith, Latin literacy, and papal authority. Anti-Semitism and persecution of Jews were recurring features.

Europe was politically fragmented, characterized by decentralized monarchies, feudalism (lords granted land to vassals in exchange for military service), and the manorial system (self-sufficient agricultural estates). Kings had limited power compared to their nobles. The lack of centralized states contrasted sharply with Song China or the Islamic caliphates.

Europe was largely an agricultural society dependent on free and coerced labor, including serfdom (peasants legally bound to the land, owing labor and a share of their crops to the lord). Unlike chattel slavery, serfs had some legal rights and could not be sold separately from their land.

Memory Aid: Unit 1 State-Building Comparison
Every region had states that demonstrated "CID": Continuity (with past traditions), Innovation (new methods of governance), and Diversity (adapting to local conditions). Use this as your framework when comparing any two regions.

Multiple Choice

1. Which of the following best describes a similarity between the Song Dynasty and the Abbasid Caliphate?
(A) Both relied primarily on military force to maintain political unity
(B) Both promoted intellectual and technological innovation
(C) Both used feudal systems to distribute political power
(D) Both experienced territorial expansion during this period
Answer: (B). The Song Dynasty was famous for innovations like paper money, gunpowder applications, and the compass, while the Abbasid Caliphate fostered intellectual innovation through institutions like the House of Wisdom. (A) is wrong because neither relied primarily on military force for unity. (C) is wrong because feudalism was a European system. (D) is wrong because both were actually declining/fragmenting territorially during this period.
2. The Bhakti movement in South Asia most closely parallels which development in another region?
(A) The spread of Neo-Confucianism in East Asia
(B) The expansion of the Seljuk Empire in Central Asia
(C) The growth of Sufism in the Islamic world
(D) The development of the manorial system in Europe
Answer: (C). Both the Bhakti movement and Sufism emphasized personal, emotional, devotional connection to the divine, making their respective religions more accessible to ordinary people. Both also sometimes challenged existing social hierarchies. (A) is intellectual philosophy, not devotional practice. (B) is military/political expansion. (D) is an economic system.
3. A historian studying the political systems of the Americas from 1200-1450 would most likely emphasize which of the following?
(A) American states were uniformly less complex than their Afro-Eurasian counterparts
(B) The Inca and Mexica empires developed sophisticated governance despite no contact with Afro-Eurasia
(C) Trade connections between the Americas and Afro-Eurasia influenced state development
(D) Religious authority played no role in legitimizing political power in the Americas
Answer: (B). The Inca (mit'a system, road network, quipu record-keeping) and Mexica (tribute system, urban planning, chinampas agriculture) developed highly sophisticated political systems independently of Afro-Eurasian influence. (A) is a Eurocentric distortion. (C) is incorrect because there was no significant contact. (D) is wrong because the Mexica used human sacrifice specifically for religious/political legitimacy.

Short-Answer Questions

SAQ: (a) Identify ONE similarity in how states in East Asia and Dar al-Islam legitimized their rule from 1200-1450. (b) Identify ONE difference. (c) Explain ONE reason for the difference identified in (b).

In contrast, Chinese political culture had a long tradition of unified imperial rule reinforced by Confucian ideology emphasizing a single "Son of Heaven" and the Mandate of Heaven, which made political fragmentation less acceptable.

🛤️ Unit 2: Networks of Exchange (c. 1200-1450) 8-10%

2.1 The Silk Roads

ECN

Improved commercial practices expanded the volume and geographic range of Silk Road trade. Key innovations included caravanserai (roadside inns providing rest, resupply, and trade opportunities), bills of exchange (early credit instruments eliminating the need to carry cash), banking houses, and paper money (pioneered by Song China). Trading cities like Kashgar and Samarkand grew powerful.

Luxury goods drove interregional trade: Chinese, Persian, and Indian artisans expanded production of textiles and porcelains for export. Iron and steel manufacturing expanded in China. These were high-value, low-bulk goods ideal for overland transport.

2.2 The Mongol Empire and the Modern World

GOV ECN CDI

Existing empires collapsed and were replaced by the Mongol khanates (the largest contiguous land empire in history, founded by Genghis Khan c. 1206). After Genghis Khan's death, the empire split into four khanates: the Golden Horde (Russia/Eastern Europe), the Chagatai Khanate (Central Asia), the Ilkhanate (Persia/Middle East), and the Yuan Dynasty (China).

Mongol expansion facilitated Afro-Eurasian trade through the Pax Mongolica (Mongol Peace), which provided security along trade routes and drew new peoples into trade networks. Significant technological and cultural transfers occurred: Greco-Islamic medical knowledge reached Western Europe, numbering systems spread, the Uyghur script was adopted for Mongol writing. The Mongols also caused massive destruction and population decline during conquests.

2.3 Exchange in the Indian Ocean

ECN CDI ENV

Technological innovations expanded Indian Ocean trade: the compass, astrolabe, and larger ship designs. This fostered the growth of states like the Swahili Coast city-states (Kilwa, Mombasa, Mogadishu), Gujarat (western India), and the Sultanate of Malacca (controlling the strategic strait between Sumatra and Malay Peninsula).

Merchants established diasporic communities where they introduced their own cultural traditions into indigenous cultures: Arab and Persian communities in East Africa, Chinese merchant communities in Southeast Asia, Malay communities across the Indian Ocean basin. These communities facilitated trade and cultural exchange in both directions.

Trade expansion depended on environmental knowledge, especially understanding of monsoon winds (blowing northeast in winter and southwest in summer), which allowed predictable seasonal sailing.

Zheng He's voyages (1405-1433): Ming Dynasty admiral led seven massive maritime expeditions with fleets of hundreds of ships through Southeast Asia, India, Arabia, and East Africa. These demonstrated Chinese naval power and facilitated cultural transfers, but were ultimately discontinued as the Ming turned inward.

2.4 Trans-Saharan Trade

TEC GOV

The camel saddle and organized caravans enabled trade across the harsh Sahara Desert, connecting West Africa to North Africa and the Mediterranean. Primary goods: gold (from West Africa) was exchanged for salt (from Saharan mines) and manufactured goods from North Africa.

The Mali Empire (c. 1235-1600) under rulers like Mansa Musa (r. 1312-1337) became enormously wealthy from controlling gold and salt trade. Mansa Musa's famous 1324 hajj to Mecca, during which he distributed so much gold he caused inflation in Cairo, demonstrated Mali's wealth to the world. The city of Timbuktu became a major center of Islamic scholarship.

2.5-2.6 Cultural and Environmental Consequences of Connectivity

CDI ENV

Cultural diffusion intensified: Buddhism spread further into East and Southeast Asia. Hinduism and Buddhism reached deeper into Southeast Asia. Islam expanded into sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South/Southeast Asia, often through Sufi missionaries and merchants rather than military conquest. Gunpowder and paper spread from China. Literary and artistic traditions crossed borders.

Notable travelers documented the interconnected world: Ibn Battuta (Moroccan Muslim, traveled ~75,000 miles across the Islamic world, 1325-1354), Margery Kempe (English Christian mystic who traveled on pilgrimage), Marco Polo (Venetian merchant who spent 17 years at the court of Kublai Khan, sparking European interest in Asia).

The urbanization of trading cities waxed and waned: some cities boomed while others declined based on shifting trade patterns.

Crops diffused along trade routes: bananas spread to Africa, new rice varieties reached East Asia, citrus spread through the Mediterranean. But so did pathogens: the bubonic plague (Black Death) spread from Central Asia along trade routes, killing roughly one-third of Europe's population (1347-1351) and devastating societies across Afro-Eurasia.

👑 Unit 3: Land-Based Empires (c. 1450-1750) 12-15%

3.1 Empires Expand

GOV

Imperial expansion relied on gunpowder, cannons, and armed trade. The four major "Gunpowder Empires": Ottoman (Southern Europe, Middle East, North Africa; peaked under Suleiman the Magnificent), Safavid (Persia; established Shi'a Islam as state religion under Shah Ismail), Mughal (South/Central Asia; established by Babur at Battle of Panipat, 1526), Manchu/Qing (Central/East Asia; conquered Ming China in 1644). Additionally, the Songhai Empire in West Africa expanded through military conquest and controlled trans-Saharan trade.

Political and religious disputes led to rivalries: Safavid-Mughal conflict over territory, Ottoman-Safavid rivalry intensifying the Sunni-Shi'a split, Songhai's conflict with Morocco (Moroccan invasion destroyed the Songhai Empire in 1591).

3.2 Empires: Administration

GOV

Bureaucratic elites and military professionals maintained centralized control: Ottoman devshirme (Christian boys recruited, converted, trained as Janissaries or administrators), salaried samurai in Tokugawa Japan, Mughal mansabdar system ranking officials by military obligations.

Religious ideas, art, and monumental architecture legitimized rule: Mexica human sacrifice (maintaining cosmic order), European divine right (monarchs ruling by God's authority), Songhai promotion of Islam, Qing imperial portraits, Incan sun temple at Cuzco, Mughal Taj Mahal, European palaces like Versailles (Louis XIV's palace symbolizing absolute power).

Innovative tax collection: Mughal zamindar system (landholders collected taxes from peasants), Ottoman tax farming (auctioning tax-collection rights to private individuals), Mexica tribute lists (recorded exactly what conquered peoples owed), Ming requirement of paying taxes in hard currency (forcing peasants into commercial markets).

3.3 Empires: Belief Systems

CDI

The Protestant Reformation (begun by Martin Luther's 95 Theses, 1517) challenged Catholic Church authority over salvation, papal power, and the sale of indulgences. This led to new denominations (Lutheranism, Calvinism, Anglicanism). The Catholic Counter-Reformation (Council of Trent, 1545-1563; Jesuit order) reformed internal practices and launched global missionary efforts. Both reformations contributed to Christianity's growth.

Ottoman-Safavid political rivalry intensified the Sunni-Shi'a split: The Safavids made Shi'a Islam their state religion, directly opposing the Sunni Ottomans. This created a sectarian divide that persists today.

Sikhism developed in the Punjab region of South Asia through interactions between Hinduism and Islam, founded by Guru Nanak (1469-1539). It rejected the caste system and emphasized monotheism, equality, and service.

⛵ Unit 4: Transoceanic Interconnections (c. 1450-1750) 12-15%

Key Topics: Technological Innovations, Exploration, Columbian Exchange, Maritime Empires, Labor Systems, Social Hierarchies

TEC GOV ENV ECN SIO

Cross-cultural technology transfer from Classical, Islamic, and Asian worlds enabled European exploration: caravel, carrack, fluyt (ship designs), lateen sail, compass, astronomical charts. Portuguese built a global trading-post empire. Spanish sponsored Columbus (1492). English, French, Dutch sought alternative routes to Asia.

Columbian Exchange: Transfer of plants (potatoes, maize, tomatoes to Afro-Eurasia; wheat, sugar, grapes to Americas), animals (horses, pigs, cattle to Americas), and diseases (smallpox, measles, malaria devastated indigenous populations, with some areas losing 90%+ of their population). American foods became staple crops worldwide, boosting population growth. Cash crops (sugar, tobacco, cotton) grown on plantations with coerced labor.

Maritime empires: Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch, French, British. Europeans established trading posts in Africa and Asia. Some Asian states limited European access: Ming China (restricted foreign trade), Tokugawa Japan (sakoku isolationist policy). African states like Asante and Kingdom of Kongo grew through maritime trade. Existing Indian Ocean trade networks continued to flourish despite European disruption.

Labor systems: Colonial economies used the Incan mit'a (adapted for silver mining at Potosi), chattel slavery, indentured servitude, encomienda (Spanish grant of indigenous labor), and hacienda systems. Traditional enslavement in Africa continued while the Atlantic slave trade massively increased demand.

Economic systems: Mercantilism guided European rulers (accumulate wealth through favorable trade balances). Joint-stock companies (Dutch/British East India Companies) financed exploration and trade. Global silver flow from Spanish Americas (especially Potosi) purchased Asian goods and satisfied Chinese demand for silver. Atlantic trading system moved goods, wealth, and enslaved persons in triangular patterns.

Social changes: Casta system formed in Americas (racial hierarchy). States accommodated ethnic/religious diversity (Ottoman millet system, Mughal tolerance under Akbar) or suppressed it (Spanish expulsion of Jews, Qing restrictions on Han Chinese). Resistance to state power: Pueblo Revolt (1680), Maroon societies, Metacom's War.

⚙️ Unit 5: Revolutions (c. 1750-1900) 12-15%

Key Topics: Enlightenment, Revolutions, Industrial Revolution, Technology, Economic Changes, Social Reactions

CDI GOV TEC ECN SIO ENV

The Enlightenment applied empiricism and reason to the natural world and human relationships. Key ideas: natural rights (Locke: life, liberty, property), social contract (Rousseau: government by consent of the governed), separation of powers (Montesquieu). These ideas influenced reform movements (expanded suffrage, abolition, end of serfdom) and emerging feminism (Wollstonecraft, de Gouges, Seneca Falls Convention 1848).

Nationalism and Revolutions: People developed shared identity based on language, religion, customs, territory. Key revolutions: American (1776, Declaration of Independence, model republic), French (1789, "Declaration of Rights of Man"), Haitian (1791-1804, Toussaint L'Ouverture, first Black republic), Latin American (Bolivar's "Letter from Jamaica," independence from Spain). National unification movements: German and Italian unifications. Balkan nationalisms challenged Ottoman control.

Industrial Revolution origins: Began in Britain due to: proximity to waterways, coal/iron/timber resources, urbanization, improved agriculture (crop rotation, enclosure movement), legal protection of private property, access to colonial resources, accumulated capital. Factory system concentrated production and specialized labor. Spread to northwestern Europe, U.S., Russia, Japan. Middle Eastern and Asian share of global manufacturing declined.

Key technologies: Steam engine (Watt), internal combustion engine, coal and oil (fossil fuels revolution). "Second Industrial Revolution" (late 1800s): steel, chemicals, electricity, precision machinery. Railroads, steamships, telegraph connected interior regions globally.

Government and economics: Some states sponsored industrialization: Muhammad Ali (Egypt, cotton textiles), Meiji Japan (rapid modernization after 1868 to prevent colonization). Western Europe shifted from mercantilism to laissez-faire capitalism (Adam Smith). Transnational businesses grew (HSBC, Unilever). New financial instruments: stock markets, limited-liability corporations.

Social reactions: Workers organized in labor unions. Karl Marx developed communist theory (class struggle between bourgeoisie and proletariat). Ottoman Empire and Qing China attempted reforms (Tanzimat, Self-Strengthening Movement) but faced elite resistance. New social classes: middle class (bourgeoisie) and industrial working class (proletariat). Working-class women/children held wage jobs; middle-class women restricted to domestic roles. Rapid urbanization caused pollution, poverty, crime, housing shortages, public health crises.

🏭 Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization (c. 1750-1900) 12-15%

Key Topics: Imperialism Rationales, State Expansion, Indigenous Resistance, Economic Imperialism, Migration

CDI GOV ECN ENV SIO

Ideological justifications for imperialism: Social Darwinism (misapplying "survival of the fittest" to races/nations), nationalism (national prestige required colonies), "civilizing mission" (duty to spread Western civilization), religious conversion (Christian missionaries). The Berlin Conference (1884-85) formalized the "Scramble for Africa" with no African participation.

State expansion: European states, U.S., and Japan expanded empires. Non-state to state transitions: Leopold II's private Congo to Belgian government, Dutch East India Company to Dutch government in Indonesia. European empires in Africa: Britain (West Africa, Egypt, South Africa), Belgium (Congo), France (West Africa, Algeria). Settler colonies established (New Zealand, Australia, South Africa). U.S. expanded westward (Manifest Destiny), Russia expanded into Central Asia, Japan expanded into Korea and Taiwan (after Meiji Restoration).

Indigenous responses varied: Direct resistance (Tupac Amaru II in Peru, Samory Toure in West Africa, Yaa Asantewaa War, 1857 Indian rebellion). New states formed (Sokoto Caliphate, Zulu Kingdom, Cherokee Nation, Balkan independence). Religiously-influenced rebellions (Ghost Dance in U.S., Xhosa Cattle-Killing Movement, Mahdist wars in Sudan, Boxer Rebellion in China).

Economic imperialism: Export economies grew worldwide (rubber, cotton, palm oil, guano, diamonds, meat). Industrialized states practiced economic imperialism in Asia and Latin America: Opium Wars forced China to open ports and cede Hong Kong. Trade was organized to give European/American merchants distinct advantages.

Migration patterns shifted: Demographic changes, new transportation, and urbanization drove migration. Many moved freely seeking work (Irish to U.S., Italians to Argentina). Others were coerced (Chinese/Indian indentured servitude, convict labor to Australia). New transportation enabled return migration. Male migrants left women to assume new roles. Ethnic enclaves formed (Chinatowns, Little Italys). Receiving societies responded with prejudice and regulation (Chinese Exclusion Act 1882, White Australia Policy 1901).

Ukrainian Holodomor under Stalin (1932-33, ~3.5-7 million died in engineered famine).

Multiple Choice

1. The collapse of the Ottoman, Russian, and Qing empires in the early 20th century shared which common cause?
(A) All three were defeated in World War I
(B) All three faced a combination of internal weaknesses and external pressures that undermined their ability to govern
(C) All three were overthrown by communist revolutions
(D) All three voluntarily dissolved to create democratic successor states
Answer: (B). All three empires faced internal weaknesses (corruption, inability to modernize, ethnic/nationalist tensions) combined with external pressures (military defeats, economic competition from industrialized states). (A) is wrong because the Qing fell before WWI (1912). (C) is wrong because only Russia had a communist revolution. (D) is wrong because the collapses were not voluntary.

Short-Answer Question

SAQ: (a) Identify ONE cause of World War I. (b) Explain how the conduct of World War I differed from previous wars. (c) Identify ONE cause of World War II that was a direct result of World War I.

(a) Cause: A flawed alliance system in Europe divided the great powers into two opposing blocs: the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (France, Russia, Britain). This meant that a local conflict (the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo, 1914) triggered a chain reaction of alliance obligations that rapidly escalated into a continent-wide and then global war.

(b) Conduct: WWI was the first total war, meaning that entire national economies, populations, and resources were mobilized for the war effort. Previous wars were fought primarily by professional armies with limited civilian involvement. WWI saw governments use political propaganda, censorship, and intensified nationalism to mobilize civilian populations, including women (who entered factories) and colonial subjects (recruited for labor and combat). New military technologies (machine guns, poison gas, tanks, airplanes, submarines) produced unprecedented casualty rates.

(c) Result leading to WWII: The Treaty of Versailles (1919) imposed punitive terms on Germany, including massive reparations, territorial losses (Alsace-Lorraine, colonies), military restrictions, and a "war guilt" clause. These harsh terms humiliated Germany, devastated its economy, and created deep resentment that Hitler and the Nazi Party exploited to gain popular support. The Treaty's unsustainability was a direct cause of WWII.

🕊️ Unit 8: Cold War & Decolonization (c. 1900-present) 8-10%

Key Topics: Cold War Origins, Proxy Wars, Communism, Decolonization, New States, Resistance, End of Cold War

GOV CDI ECN SIO

Cold War origins: After WWII, the U.S. (capitalism/democracy) and Soviet Union (communism/authoritarianism) emerged as superpowers. Ideological conflict and power struggle produced the Cold War (1947-1991). Military alliances formed: NATO (Western) and Warsaw Pact (Soviet). Nuclear proliferation created threat of mutual destruction. The Non-Aligned Movement (Sukarno, Nkrumah, Nehru, Nasser) sought alternatives to both blocs.

Proxy wars: Superpowers supported opposing sides without direct confrontation: Korean War (1950-53), Vietnam War (1955-75), Angolan Civil War, Sandinista-Contras in Nicaragua, Soviet-Afghan War (1979-89).

Spread of communism: Chinese communists seized power (1949) under Mao Zedong. Great Leap Forward (1958-62) attempted rapid industrialization through collectivization but caused catastrophic famine (~15-45 million deaths). Movements to redistribute land/resources developed in Africa, Asia, Latin America (Vietnam, Ethiopia, Kerala/India).

Decolonization: Nationalist leaders sought independence: Indian National Congress (Gandhi, Nehru), Ho Chi Minh (Vietnam), Kwame Nkrumah (Ghana), Nasser (Egypt). Independence came through negotiation (India 1947, Gold Coast/Ghana 1957, French West Africa) or armed struggle (Algeria, Angola, Vietnam). Regional/religious/ethnic movements challenged colonial boundaries (Muslim League/Partition of India, Biafra in Nigeria).

Newly independent states: Redrawing borders created new states (Israel, Cambodia, Pakistan) and sometimes conflict/displacement (Partition of India, creation of Israel). Governments guided economic development (Nasser in Egypt, Nyerere in Tanzania, Indira Gandhi in India). Former colonial subjects migrated to imperial metropoles (South Asians to Britain, Algerians to France).

Resistance and End of Cold War: Nonviolent leaders: Gandhi, MLK Jr., Nelson Mandela. Militarized responses intensified conflict: Pinochet (Chile), Franco (Spain), Idi Amin (Uganda). Violent movements: Shining Path, Al-Qaeda. The Cold War ended due to U.S. military/technological advantages, Soviet Union's failed Afghan invasion, and public discontent/economic weakness in communist countries. Soviet Union collapsed 1991.

Multiple Choice

1. The Non-Aligned Movement is best understood as an attempt by newly independent nations to
(A) form a military alliance stronger than NATO and the Warsaw Pact
(B) avoid choosing sides in the Cold War while pursuing independent development
(C) establish communist governments throughout the developing world
(D) return to pre-colonial political structures and reject all Western influence
Answer: (B). The Non-Aligned Movement, led by figures like Sukarno (Indonesia), Nkrumah (Ghana), Nehru (India), and Nasser (Egypt), explicitly rejected alignment with either the U.S. or Soviet bloc. These nations sought to chart independent paths of development without being forced to choose sides in the superpower rivalry. It was not a military alliance (A), not pro-communist (C), and did not reject all modernization (D).

Short-Answer Question

SAQ: (a) Describe ONE way decolonization in Asia differed from decolonization in Africa. (b) Identify ONE challenge faced by newly independent states after decolonization. (c) Explain how the Cold War influenced the process of decolonization.

(a) Difference: In Asia, decolonization often involved larger, more established nationalist movements with clear political organizations (Indian National Congress founded in 1885, decades before independence in 1947), while many African independence movements developed more rapidly in the post-WWII period. Additionally, Asian decolonization often involved partition along religious lines (India/Pakistan, North/South Vietnam, North/South Korea), while African decolonization more often maintained colonial-era borders that cut across ethnic groups.

(b) Challenge: Newly independent states faced the challenge of inheriting artificial colonial borders that grouped together diverse ethnic, linguistic, and religious communities who had not chosen to be part of the same nation. This led to internal conflicts, secessionist movements (Biafra in Nigeria), and ethnic tensions (Tutsi and Hutu in Rwanda/Burundi) that colonial powers had often deliberately exploited through divide-and-rule strategies.

(c) Cold War influence: The Cold War shaped decolonization by turning newly independent states into proxy battlegrounds for superpower competition. Both the U.S. and Soviet Union provided military aid, economic support, and political backing to friendly regimes in the developing world, regardless of whether those regimes were democratic. This meant that some independence movements received support based on Cold War alignment rather than democratic principles (e.g., U.S. support for anti-communist dictators, Soviet support for Marxist liberation movements), often prolonging conflicts and undermining genuine self-determination.

🌐 Unit 9: Globalization (c. 1900-present) 8-10%

Key Topics: Technology, Disease, Environment, Global Economy, Social Reform, Culture, Resistance, International Institutions

TEC ENV ECN SIO CDI GOV

Technology transformed everything: Communication (radio, cell phones, internet) and transportation (air travel, shipping containers) reduced geographic barriers. Energy (petroleum, nuclear power) raised productivity. Green Revolution sustained growing populations through chemically/genetically modified agriculture. Medical innovations (vaccines, antibiotics) extended lifespans. Birth control gave women control over fertility, contributing to declining fertility rates.

Disease: Poverty-associated diseases persisted (malaria, tuberculosis, cholera). New epidemics emerged (1918 influenza pandemic, Ebola, HIV/AIDS). Diseases of longevity (heart disease, Alzheimer's) increased as lifespans grew.

Environmental challenges: Human activity caused deforestation, desertification, declining air quality, freshwater depletion. Greenhouse gases sparked debates about climate change. Competition over resources intensified.

Global economy: Many governments embraced free-market policies (Reagan/U.S., Thatcher/Britain, Deng Xiaoping/China, Pinochet/Chile). Knowledge economies grew (Finland, Japan, U.S.) while manufacturing shifted to Asia/Latin America (Vietnam, Bangladesh, Mexico). Trade agreements: WTO, NAFTA, ASEAN. Multinational corporations expanded globally (Nestle, Nissan, Mahindra).

Social reform: Rights-based movements challenged assumptions about race, class, gender, religion: UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948), global feminism, Negritude movement, liberation theology. Access to education and political roles expanded: women's suffrage worldwide, U.S. Civil Rights Act, end of apartheid, caste reservation in India. Environmental movements: Greenpeace, Wangari Maathai's Green Belt Movement.

Globalized culture: Popular culture became global through technology: music (Reggae), movies (Bollywood), social media (Facebook/Twitter), TV (BBC), sports (World Cup, Olympics). Consumer culture transcended borders: online commerce (Alibaba, eBay), global brands (Toyota, Coca-Cola).

Resistance to globalization: Anti-IMF/World Bank activism, locally developed social media (Weibo in China), cultural protectionism, nationalist backlash. International institutions: United Nations founded to maintain peace and facilitate cooperation.

Multiple Choice

1. The shift of manufacturing from developed nations to countries like Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Mexico in the late 20th century most directly resulted from
(A) government mandates requiring companies to relocate production
(B) the spread of free-market economic policies and the search for lower labor costs
(C) workers in developed nations refusing to perform manufacturing jobs
(D) international treaties requiring equal distribution of manufacturing capacity
Answer: (B). The trend of economic liberalization (promoted by leaders like Reagan, Thatcher, and Deng Xiaoping), combined with trade agreements (WTO, NAFTA) that reduced barriers, allowed multinational corporations to relocate production to countries with lower labor costs and fewer regulations. This was a market-driven process, not a government mandate (A) or treaty obligation (D).
2. Which of the following best represents a continuity in world history from the period of the Columbian Exchange (1450-1750) to the modern era of globalization (1900-present)?
(A) Increased global connectivity has consistently led to both benefits and unintended negative consequences
(B) Trade has consistently been controlled by a single dominant global power
(C) Technological change has consistently benefited all populations equally
(D) Cultural exchange has consistently led to the complete replacement of local traditions with Western culture
Answer: (A). This is a key continuity across all periods. The Columbian Exchange brought nutritional benefits (new food crops) but also devastating disease. Modern globalization brings economic growth and cultural exchange but also environmental degradation, inequality, and cultural homogenization concerns. In both periods, increased connectivity produced both positive and negative outcomes. (B), (C), and (D) are historically inaccurate.

Short-Answer Question

SAQ: (a) Describe ONE way technology changed daily life in the 20th century. (b) Describe ONE way globalization changed culture after 1900. (c) Explain ONE way groups or individuals resisted globalization.

(a) Technology: The development of birth control (especially the oral contraceptive pill, introduced in the 1960s) fundamentally changed daily life by giving women greater control over fertility. This transformed reproductive practices worldwide, contributed to declining birth rates in many countries, enabled women to pursue education and careers, and reshaped family structures. It was one of the most personally impactful technological changes of the century.

(b) Culture: Popular and consumer culture became increasingly globalized through technology. For example, Bollywood (India's Hindi-language film industry) reached global audiences, becoming one of the world's largest film industries by output. Bollywood's global popularity demonstrated that cultural globalization was not simply Americanization or Westernization but involved cultural products from many regions reaching international audiences through new distribution technologies.

(c) Resistance: China developed its own social media platforms (Weibo, WeChat) partly as a response to globalized Western platforms like Facebook and Twitter. The Chinese government blocked access to many Western platforms behind the "Great Firewall," promoting domestic alternatives. This represented resistance to both the cultural influence of Western technology companies and the political implications of unrestricted information flow, demonstrating how states could use technology to resist aspects of globalization while still participating in the global economy.
